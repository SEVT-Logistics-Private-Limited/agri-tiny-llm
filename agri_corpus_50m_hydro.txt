Hydroponic systems fall into six main categories, each with different tradeoffs.
Wick systems are passive, using a wick to draw nutrient solution up into the growing
medium, suited only to small, low-water-demand plants. Deep Water Culture suspends
roots directly in aerated solution. Nutrient Film Technique flows a thin film
continuously over roots in a sloped channel. Ebb and Flow floods a grow tray
periodically then drains it back to a reservoir. Drip systems deliver solution
directly at the base of each plant on a timed cycle. The Kratky method is a
non-circulating passive method where a fixed volume of solution is provided upfront
and the water level drops as the plant grows, exposing more roots to air over time
without needing pumps at all.

calcium and magnesium can precipitate out of solution at pH above 7.0, forming a
white or gray sediment in reservoirs and clogging drip emitters over time.

Q: My hydroponic system pH is 4.2, what should I do?
A: A pH of 4.2 is too acidic for almost all hydroponic crops. Add a pH-up solution gradually and retest until the pH reaches 5.5 to 6.5.

Q: What does potassium deficiency look like in a hydroponic plant?
A: Potassium deficiency typically appears as yellowing or browning at the leaf margins while the center of the leaf stays green, sometimes called leaf margin scorch.

For most leafy greens grown hydroponically, the ideal pH range is between 5.5 and
6.5. Outside this range, nutrient uptake becomes inefficient even if the correct
nutrients are present in solution, because certain minerals become chemically locked
and unavailable to the roots at high or low pH.

The Kratky method works because as water level drops, an air gap forms above the
solution, giving roots access to atmospheric oxygen without any aeration equipment.
This makes it popular for small-scale, low-tech growing, though it is generally

crops that need continuous nutrient replenishment.

Nitrogen deficiency causes uniform yellowing starting with older, lower leaves,
since nitrogen is mobile and the plant relocates it from old tissue to new growth.
Phosphorus deficiency often shows as dark green or purplish leaves, especially on
the undersides, and stunted growth. Potassium deficiency appears as yellowing or
browning at leaf edges (leaf margin scorch) while the leaf center remains green.
Sulfur deficiency resembles nitrogen deficiency but affects new growth first, since
sulfur is far less mobile within the plant than nitrogen.

A: Yellowing of older leaves first is a classic sign of nitrogen deficiency in the nutrient solution.

Source water quality affects hydroponic success before any nutrients are added.
Municipal tap water often contains chlorine or chloramine, which can be removed by

avoid oversupplying those elements, while reverse osmosis water, having nearly zero
mineral content, requires a complete nutrient mix including calcium and magnesium
that plain tap water might partially already supply.

water solution to deliver minerals directly to the roots. Common inert growing media

Micronutrient deficiencies are usually diagnosed by looking at which part of the
plant shows symptoms first. Iron and manganese deficiencies both cause interveinal
yellowing on new leaves, since neither is mobile, but iron deficiency tends to leave
very fine, sharply defined green veins while manganese deficiency produces a more
blotchy, less defined pattern. Zinc deficiency causes shortened stem internodes and
small, narrow new leaves (rosetting). Boron deficiency shows first at growing tips,
causing them to die back, since boron is needed for new cell wall formation and
cannot be relocated from older tissue.

Light is measured for hydroponic crops using PPFD (photosynthetic photon flux

the total light delivered over a day). Leafy greens generally need a DLI of 12 to 17

yields. Light spectrum also matters: blue light promotes compact, leafy growth while
red light promotes stem elongation and flowering, which is why many grow lights blend
both rather than using pure white light.

Hydroponic systems fall into six main categories, each with different tradeoffs.
Wick systems are passive, using a wick to draw nutrient solution up into the growing
medium, suited only to small, low-water-demand plants. Deep Water Culture suspends
roots directly in aerated solution. Nutrient Film Technique flows a thin film
continuously over roots in a sloped channel. Ebb and Flow floods a grow tray
periodically then drains it back to a reservoir. Drip systems deliver solution
directly at the base of each plant on a timed cycle. The Kratky method is a
non-circulating passive method where a fixed volume of solution is provided upfront
and the water level drops as the plant grows, exposing more roots to air over time
without needing pumps at all.

peppers, eggplant, cucumbers, melons) need higher-flow systems with strong aeration
and physical support, typically drip or ebb and flow with trellising, since their
root systems and nutrient demand outgrow what passive systems can sustain.

Micronutrient deficiencies are usually diagnosed by looking at which part of the
plant shows symptoms first. Iron and manganese deficiencies both cause interveinal
yellowing on new leaves, since neither is mobile, but iron deficiency tends to leave
very fine, sharply defined green veins while manganese deficiency produces a more
blotchy, less defined pattern. Zinc deficiency causes shortened stem internodes and
small, narrow new leaves (rosetting). Boron deficiency shows first at growing tips,
causing them to die back, since boron is needed for new cell wall formation and
cannot be relocated from older tissue.

Growing media each behave differently with water and air. Rockwool holds high
water content with good aeration and is common for seed starting and as a slab medium
in drip systems. Perlite is lightweight, volcanic glass that provides excellent
drainage and aeration but low water retention. Expanded clay pebbles (LECA) are
reusable, pH-neutral, and provide strong aeration, common in ebb and flow and DWC net
pots. Coco coir retains moisture well while still allowing airflow, and is close to
pH-neutral once properly buffered. Vermiculite retains far more water than perlite
and is often blended with it to balance moisture and aeration.

For most leafy greens grown hydroponically, the ideal pH range is between 5.5 and
6.5. Outside this range, nutrient uptake becomes inefficient even if the correct
nutrients are present in solution, because certain minerals become chemically locked
and unavailable to the roots at high or low pH.

Q: Why does root rot suddenly appear after weeks of healthy growth?
A: Rising water temperature lowers dissolved oxygen capacity while increasing root oxygen demand at the same time, so a hydroponic system that was stable for weeks can tip into root rot quickly during a hot spell.

The Kratky method works because as water level drops, an air gap forms above the

Light is measured for hydroponic crops using PPFD (photosynthetic photon flux
density, in micromoles per square meter per second) and DLI (daily light integral,
the total light delivered over a day). Leafy greens generally need a DLI of 12 to 17

A complete hydroponic nutrient solution must supply six macronutrients: nitrogen,

sulfate can react with phosphate to form insoluble precipitates, most commercial

Q: Is TDS the same thing as pH in hydroponics?
A: No, TDS measures the concentration of dissolved nutrients in the water (in ppm or EC), while pH measures acidity or alkalinity on a 0 to 14 scale; both need to be correct, but they are measured and adjusted independently.

Hydroponic systems fall into six main categories, each with different tradeoffs.
Wick systems are passive, using a wick to draw nutrient solution up into the growing
medium, suited only to small, low-water-demand plants. Deep Water Culture suspends
roots directly in aerated solution. Nutrient Film Technique flows a thin film

non-circulating passive method where a fixed volume of solution is provided upfront
